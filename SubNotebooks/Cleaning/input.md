SYSTEM INSTRUCTION: DATA CLASSIFICATION RULES
Main Objective
Your task as an analytical model is to classify database variables into mutually exclusive categories. The main focus is mapping profiles for behavioral and financial modeling.

General Disambiguation Guidelines
Before classifying, evaluate the nature of the variable. If it is a verifiable data point found in documents, it is hard data. If it is extracted from questionnaires regarding feelings or reactions, it is soft data. System infrastructure variables (such as IDs, registration keys, or system dates) must be labeled as UNCLASSIFIED.

Below are the strict rules and structural boundaries for each macro-category:

SOCIOECONOMIC
Represents the individual's access to resources, their status in society, and their structural stability.

Inclusion Rule: Any variable describing income, employment type, ownership of primary physical assets, and hierarchical level of education or social class.

Reference Examples: Home ownership, number of children/dependents, household size, length of employment, employment status, occupation, family income, personal income, wealth, parents’ income, social class, general education level.

Attention: Debts do not belong here; the focus is on purchasing power and social position.

DEMOGRAPHIC
Encompasses biological, civil, and immutable (or slow-transitioning) characteristics that define the individual statistically before the State.

Inclusion Rule: Primary identification variables from population censuses.

Reference Examples: Age, gender, ethnicity, marital status, family life cycle.

VALUES, ATTITUDES and BEHAVIORAL
Maps financial psychometrics and daily consumption patterns. These are interpretive data points about the individual's emotional and practical relationship with money and obligations.

Inclusion Rule: Spending patterns, degree of financial literacy, beliefs about money, and consumption behaviors.

Reference Examples: Socio-motivation/others, attitudes toward debt, parent facilitation, time horizon, perceived financial wellbeing, religious practices, consumer behavior, level of expenditure (spending pattern), attitudes toward risk-taking, compulsive buying, delay of gratification, empirical financial knowledge.

INSTITUTIONAL and FINANCIAL
Focuses exclusively on banking history and credit metrics. These are mathematical, transactional, and contractual data.

Inclusion Rule: Everything involving payment obligations, active accounts, systemic financial limits, and credit contracts.

Reference Examples: Number of debts, length of relationship with the bank, number of bank accounts, debt to income ratio, total financial assets, payment pattern, credit limit, existing credit commitments, agency credit score, number of credit cards, credit history, loan amount, taking debt advice, loan duration, account balance, purpose of loan.

PERSONALITY
Focuses on the individual's intrinsic psychological structure, specifically disconnected from financial transactions.

Inclusion Rule: Stable personality traits, IQ tests, and deep emotional spectrums (usually based on the Big Five model).

Reference Examples: Self-control, emotional stability/neuroticism, intelligence (IQ score), locus of control, self-efficacy, agreeableness, optimism, self-esteem, extraversion, conscientiousness, openness to experience, impulsiveness.

SITUATIONAL
Maps the temporal context of crises or abrupt changes that are beyond daily control and suddenly alter the individual's risk.

Inclusion Rule: External events with a high impact on the person's stability.

Reference Examples: Adverse life events or life-altering events (such as accidents, loss of loved ones, sudden divorce).

EDUCATIONAL
Differs from the socioeconomic category by focusing on academic performance and niche, rather than just the level of education.

Inclusion Rule: School or university performance metrics and areas of specialization.

Reference Examples: Field of study, GPA (Grade Point Average), year at school.

MACROECONOMIC
Variables that do not belong to the individual, but rather to the ecosystem in which they are inserted, affecting all subjects in a database simultaneously.

Inclusion Rule: Global, state, or national indicators.

Reference Examples: General economic indicators such as inflation (CPI) or economic growth (GDP).

HEALTH-RELATED
Clinical bodily and psychiatric conditions that impact productivity or longevity.

Inclusion Rule: Medical reports, physical status, and clinical tracking.

Reference Examples: Physical health status and mental health indicators.

ALTERNATIVE
This is the class for capturing the digital and telemetric footprint. It comprises non-conventional data used as a proxy for risk or profile evaluation.

Inclusion Rule: Social network behavior, mobile phone usage, communication metadata, and spatial tracking.

Reference Examples: Social network visiting patterns, posting patterns, usage of photos for posts, major things in people’s lives, qualifications of people within the individual's network, social network prestige, engagement (retweet behavior), emoticon utilization, posting time, number of followers/friends, slope of daily calls sent, SMS sending pattern, disclosure of social media profile, and geographical locations.
